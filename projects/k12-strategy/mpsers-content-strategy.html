Psychology framework

The research principles that govern how this content should be sequenced and delivered
Principle 01
Extended Parallel Process Model (EPPM)
Fear only produces behavior change when paired with efficacy. Fear alone produces paralysis and tab-closing. The required sequence is: reveal the threat → restore a sense of control → provide the specific action. Every alarming section needs an immediate exit ramp. The calculator is the most critical efficacy tool — it converts abstract fear into a specific, manageable number.
Principle 02
Identity before information
People don't process generic information well. They process information that applies to them. The hire date gate is the most important UX element on the site. Everything after it should feel written specifically for this person. Sections irrelevant to their tier should disappear. Retention triples when readers feel the content is about them specifically.
Principle 03
Curiosity gaps drive reading
The K12 quiz hook works because it opens a loop the brain must close. Every section headline should operate the same way. "What happens to your health insurance the day you retire?" demands an answer. "Retiree Healthcare Benefits" is a filing cabinet label. Headlines are questions or incomplete thoughts — never topic labels.
Principle 04
The peak-end rule
Readers remember two moments above all others: the most emotionally intense moment and the final moment. The pay gap is the peak — it hits early and hard with official data. The ending must be empowering, not alarming. The action plan and advisor-vetting section close the loop with agency, not anxiety. Never end on fear.
Principle 05
Progressive disclosure
Cognitive overload is the enemy of comprehension. Reveal complexity gradually — hook, then identity, then the most relevant alarming fact, then the mechanics that explain it, then the actions that address it. Every primary section should be readable in 90 seconds. Depth lives behind the expand button, not in front of it.
Principle 06
Peer protection share trigger
Michigan educators share things when they feel the urgent need to protect a colleague. "You need to see this" is the motivation. The most shareable moments are the alarming revelations — DC-only trap, healthcare gap, no-COLA reality. Design those moments to be screenshot-worthy and easy to forward. Specific dollar figures, not ranges.
Principle 07
Narrative transportation
When readers are absorbed in a story, resistance to new information drops significantly. The anonymous insider framing, the "nobody told you this" adversarial-toward-system voice, and the use of specific dollar examples (not ranges, not approximations) all trigger narrative transportation. This site tells a story — the reader's story — not a reference manual.
Principle 08
Sunk cost of reading
Once a reader has invested time in content — answered a quiz question, entered their hire date, run the calculator — they are significantly more likely to continue reading. Each interactive element is a commitment device. Design the early experience to generate small investments that pull readers through the full arc. The quiz and hire date gate serve this function.

→
Resolution
Check your 403(b) expense ratios today. Anything above 0.50% warrants scrutiny. FINRA BrokerCheck and SEC IAPD are free and take two minutes to use.
Alarm
The payment option decision at retirement is permanent and irrevocable. Most people make it in a rushed conversation they don't fully understand.
→
Resolution
Read all six options now — years before you need to choose. Understanding them early converts an irrevocable decision into a considered one made without pressure.
Alarm
The MPSERS system is only 74.6% funded with a $25.1B unfunded liability. Part of what you pay funds past debt, not your own pension.
→
Resolution
Your earned pension is legally protected — the UAAL is a funding problem, not a benefit-cancellation problem. But it is an argument for not depending solely on the pension, and for maximizing supplemental savings.
Alarm
Taking a contribution refund when leaving teaching temporarily forfeits your service credit — and the cost to repurchase it later is far higher than what you received.
→
Resolution
Leave your contributions with ORS if there is any chance you return to Michigan public education. Your service credit stays dormant but intact — it does not expire.

Content that is positive only — no alarm pairing needed

Good news: PA 127 of 2024
The 3% retiree healthcare member contribution was eliminated effective October 1, 2025 for premium subsidy members. Most pre-2012 educators don't know their take-home pay just increased. Present this as a win — no alarm framing needed.
Good news: Roth 457 — new September 2025
MPSERS members can now make post-tax Roth contributions to the State of Michigan 457 Plan. No income limit, no required minimum distributions, tax-free growth. One of the most significant new planning tools in years. Most members have no idea it exists.
Underappreciated value: Employer contribution
School districts contribute 27–31% of payroll to MPSERS on behalf of each teacher — money that never appears on a pay stub. A $69,000 salary has an additional $18,000–$21,000/year being contributed on the teacher's behalf. Frame as the invisible compensation most educators never account for.
Underappreciated advantage: MIP early retirement
A MIP member who starts at 22 and teaches continuously can retire at 52 with a full, unreduced pension. This is genuinely rare in American public retirement systems. Most MIP members don't fully understand what they have — frame it as a gift worth understanding and protecting.

Six questions before we build

The answers materially change structural decisions — worth resolving before writing a line of HTML
Question 01 — Audience entry point
Do you expect most visitors to arrive knowing nothing — or with a specific question already in mind?
›
This determines whether the quiz or the pay gap stat is the true opening move. If visitors arrive curious but uninvested, the quiz hook ("I bet you can't answer these 5 questions") builds commitment before the data lands. If they arrive with a specific pain point already — "I'm worried about my retirement" — the $25,375 number validates that concern before they've done anything. Both are valid; they produce different hero sections.
Impacts: hero section structure, whether the quiz comes before or after the stat, how long Phase 1 runs before the hire date gate.
Question 02 — The quiz
Is the quiz a gateway into the guide — or has the hire date diagnostic replaced it as the personalization entry point?
›
The K12 quiz was designed as the entry experience — take the quiz, feel the gap, then read the guide. The hire date diagnostic is arguably more powerful: it requires less effort, delivers immediate personal relevance, and filters all subsequent content. The quiz has high shareability and produces a score (social currency) but adds friction before the core content begins. They can coexist — quiz as engagement, hire date as personalization — or one can be primary.
Impacts: whether quiz is Step 1 or a sidebar feature, page length, mobile experience, share mechanics.
Question 03 — Architecture
Should the full journey live on a single scrolling page, or should sections be separate pages with their own URLs?
›
Single page: simpler build, simpler Cloudflare Pages deployment, one URL to share. The current site is single-file and that pattern works well. Downside: a 7-section comprehensive guide is a long scroll, and sharing a specific section requires an anchor link that not all platforms handle cleanly. Multi-page: each section has a clean URL (mpsers.guide/healthcare), better for targeted sharing and SEO, harder to maintain without a build framework. My leaning is single page with robust anchor navigation — the hire date filter keeps it from feeling overwhelming.
Impacts: Cloudflare Pages setup, shareability of specific sections, maintenance overhead, whether deep-linking works cleanly.
Question 04 — Depth vs. simplicity
Is the primary reader someone who wants to understand the full system — or someone who wants to know what applies to them and what to do about it?
›
The K12 guide goes deep on every topic — 4–6 accordion items per section, tables, edge cases, nuances. That serves a motivated research reader but creates abandon risk for a busy teacher with 8 minutes. The new site could be more opinionated: "here is what matters for your situation, here is what to do, here are the details if you want them." The 90-second-per-section rule from the psychology framework suggests simplicity as the default, with depth available. My recommendation: lead with the verdict, put the evidence behind the accordion.
Impacts: how much content lives above vs. below the fold per section, accordion density, whether secondary topics (taxes, service credit edge cases) get full sections or brief mentions with ORS links.
Question 05 — The terminal action
What do you want the reader to DO at the end of the site — and at the end of each section?
›
Currently the site has no commercial CTA, a share prompt, and a placeholder email for guide questions. That's clean but leaves motivated readers with no obvious next step except navigate away. Options: (A) email address for questions only — "Did the guide miss something? Send it here and I'll add it," (B) "Notify me when updated" email capture — one field, honest about its purpose, builds a list for future use, (C) resources section as the terminal action — miAccount, SSA.gov, ORS tools. A combination is fine. The ending shapes the entire experience. Option B is my recommendation — lowest friction, most honest, highest future value.
Impacts: footer design, per-section CTA wording, whether a Cloudflare Worker + KV email capture needs to be built into the deployment.
Question 06 — Section count
Should every topic get a full primary section — or should some be consolidated into reference material within larger sections?
›
Seven primary narrative sections with depth behind accordions is scannable and navigable on first load. Twelve equal sections feels like a textbook and creates a navigation problem — which one do I start with? My strong recommendation: 7 primary sections following the story spine, with secondary topics (taxes, system funding, service credit edge cases, ORS resource links) consolidated as a reference appendix section. The guide competes with ORS on comprehensiveness and loses. It wins on approachability, narrative, and personalization.
Impacts: nav structure, scroll length, how intimidating the page feels on first load, whether the guide complements ORS or tries to replace it.

Structural recommendation

Based on everything assembled — the proposed blueprint before you answer the six questions
The core insight: The site tries to do three things — alarm, educate, and empower. Most educational sites front-load education. This site should front-load the alarm, deliver education as the explanation for the alarm, and close with empowerment. That is the complete EPPM cycle, and it mirrors how the best investigative journalism works.
