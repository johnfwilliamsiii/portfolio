Where Does Your Money Go?

Every paycheck, a percentage of your salary is deducted and sent to MPSERS. Here's what actually happens to it.

1
What exactly is MPSERS and who runs it?
+

MPSERS stands for the Michigan Public School Employees' Retirement System — the pension and benefits program for Michigan's public school employees. It is administered by the Michigan Office of Retirement Services (ORS), a state agency under the Department of Technology, Management and Budget.

MPSERS covers all public school districts in Michigan, as well as intermediate school districts, community colleges, and certain other educational entities. It is one of the largest public pension systems in Michigan, with over 200,000 active members and more than 100,000 retirees.

ℹ
Your employer (school district) does not manage your retirement benefits — ORS does. Questions about your pension, service credit, or retirement eligibility should go to ORS, not your district's HR department.
2
What percentage of my paycheck goes to MPSERS?
+

Your contribution rate depends on your membership plan (tier). For 2024–25:

PlanEmployee Contribution Rate
Basic Plan0% (non-contributory)
MIP Fixed3.9%
MIP Graded3.0%–6.4% (salary-based tiers)
MIP Plus6.4%
MIP 7%7.0%
Pension Plus / PP2 (DB portion)6.2%
DC-Only0% to pension; 3% to DC (matched by employer)
Personal Healthcare Fund (PHF)+2% if hired 9/4/2012 or later
⚠
If you don't know which plan you're in, log into miAccount at michigan.gov/orsschools. This is the single most important thing you can do today.
3
Does my contribution sit in a personal account with my name on it?
+

For DB (defined benefit) pension members — Basic, MIP, Pension Plus, Pension Plus 2 — your contributions go into the MPSERS trust fund, which is pooled. It is not a personal account. Your eventual pension is calculated by a formula, not by how much you personally contributed.

For DC (defined contribution) members — Pension Plus, PP2, and DC-Only — a portion goes into an individual account invested in funds you select through Voya Financial. That portion is yours and grows based on investment performance.

ℹ
If you leave before vesting (10 years), you get your own DB contributions back — but not the employer's share or any pension benefit. This is why staying to vesting is so financially significant.
4
What does my employer contribute on my behalf?
+

Your school district contributes a significantly larger share to MPSERS on your behalf. Employer contribution rates often exceed 25–35% of salary when pension and healthcare obligations are combined. This is money your employer pays that never appears on your paystub.

✓
The employer contribution is one of the most underappreciated parts of your total compensation. A teacher earning $60,000 may have an employer contributing an additional $15,000–$20,000 per year toward their retirement — real value that never shows on an offer letter.

1
Why does my tier matter so much?
+

Your tier determines your pension multiplier, FAC averaging period, retirement eligibility age, retiree healthcare benefit, and how much of your salary goes to MPSERS. Two teachers in the same classroom earning the same salary can have retirement outcomes differing by hundreds of thousands of dollars over their lifetime — based solely on their tier.

⚠
MIP members hired between 1990 and February 2018 have the most favorable pension formula in the system — a 1.5% multiplier with a 3-year FAC. If you're MIP, you have something genuinely valuable. Understand what it's worth.
2
I was hired between 2018 and 2024 and never chose a plan. What am I in?
+

If you were hired between February 2018 and June 30, 2024, and did not actively choose within your 75-day election window, you were defaulted into the DC-only plan. You have no defined benefit pension. Starting July 1, 2024, new hires who don't choose are defaulted into Pension Plus 2.

⚠
This is one of the most consequential missed decisions in MPSERS. A teacher hired in 2019 who didn't read their new-hire paperwork carefully may have zero pension and not realize it until years in. Check miAccount today.
3
Can I switch plans after enrollment?
+

Generally, no. The initial election is irrevocable. If you're a DC-only member, you cannot retroactively join Pension Plus 2 unless new legislation creates an election window — and none currently exists. This is why the initial 75-day decision matters so much.

4
I work part-time or as a substitute. Am I even in MPSERS?
+

Yes. MPSERS membership is automatic and includes temporary and intermittent employees. However, earning a full year of service credit requires reaching 1,020 hours in a fiscal year. If you consistently work part-time but never hit 1,020 hours, you accrue fractional service credit — which still counts toward vesting and the pension formula.

⚠
Part-time employees who never track their hour accumulation often discover late in their career they have less service credit than expected. Monitor your annual member statement carefully.

How Is Your Pension Calculated?

Your pension is a formula. Understanding each variable is the only way to project your retirement income accurately.

Annual Pension Formula
Annual Pension = FAC × Pension Factor × Years of Service
Example: $75,000 FAC × 1.5% × 30 years = $33,750/year = $2,812.50/month
1
What is Final Average Compensation (FAC)? Critical
+

FAC is the average of your highest earning years — the single biggest lever in your pension formula.

PlanFAC Period
MIP (all sub-types)Highest 3 consecutive years of pensionable earnings
Basic PlanHighest 5 consecutive years
Pension Plus / Pension Plus 2Highest 5 consecutive years

Your FAC years don't have to be your final years — they're your highest consecutive years, which ORS identifies automatically from your full earnings record.

⚠
What counts as pensionable earnings? Regular salary does. Some supplemental pay may or may not count depending on how your district reports it. Bonuses and certain stipends typically do NOT count.
2
What pension multiplier does my plan use?
+
PlanMultiplierImpact at $70K FAC / 30 YOS
MIP (all)1.5%$31,500/year ($2,625/mo)
Basic1.25%$26,250/year ($2,187/mo)
Pension Plus / PP21.25%$26,250/year ($2,187/mo)
DC-OnlyNo pension—

The difference between 1.5% and 1.25% sounds small. Over a 30-year career it's $5,250 per year — roughly $105,000 over a 20-year retirement.

3
What percentage of my income will my pension replace?
+

A MIP member with 30 years produces 45% of FAC. With 35 years: 52.5%. Pension Plus / PP2 members at 30 years get 37.5%.

Most financial planners target 70–80% income replacement. Pension alone falls short for most MPSERS members. The gap needs to be filled with Social Security, 403(b), 457, and other income.

ℹ
Michigan public school employees do participate in Social Security — unlike teachers in many other states. Your Social Security benefit adds meaningfully to total retirement income. Always model them together.
4
Is there a cost-of-living adjustment (COLA) on my pension?
+

No — not a guaranteed annual COLA. COLAs were eliminated as part of 2012 reforms. Post-retirement increases are subject to legislative action and the funded status of the system.

⚠
A $2,500/month pension in 2025 has the purchasing power of approximately $1,850/month in 2045 assuming 1.5% annual inflation. Plan as if your pension check is fixed in nominal dollars — your supplemental savings must account for this erosion.

Service Credit — Earning, Protecting & Purchasing

Service credit is the multiplier on your pension. Every fraction matters — and you can buy more.

1
How do I earn service credit?
+

You earn one year of service credit for each fiscal year (October 1 – September 30) in which you are credited with 1,020 or more hours. You cannot earn more than one year per fiscal year.

  • Active employment at an MPSERS-reporting school
  • Approved leaves of absence (paid or contributions-covered)
  • Active duty military service (with proper documentation)
  • Purchased service credit
2
Can I purchase additional service credit — and is it worth it? High Value
+

After earning at least 2 years of actual service, you can purchase credit for repayment of refunded contributions, military service, workers' compensation periods, and reciprocal public service in other Michigan retirement systems.

⚠
Service credit purchased on or after July 1, 2008 cannot be used to qualify for retiree healthcare premium subsidy. It still counts toward your pension — but not toward healthcare benefit vesting.

Is it worth it? Use the TDP Calculator on the ORS website. If adding one year increases your annual pension by $1,200 and costs $8,000, your break-even is ~6.7 years of retirement — often a good deal.

3
How many years do I need to be vested?
+
Benefit TypeVesting Requirement
DB Pension (Basic/MIP/Pension Plus/PP2)10 years of service credit
DC employer match (Pension Plus/PP2/DC)Graded: 50% at 2 yrs, 75% at 3 yrs, 100% at 4 yrs
Retiree healthcare subsidy (hired before 7/1/2008)10 years of service
Retiree healthcare (hired 9/4/12+)No subsidy — PHF only

Before 10 years, you are entitled to a refund of your own contributions (with interest) — but not a future pension benefit.

4
What happens if I leave and come back later?
+

Your accrued service credit does not disappear if you leave and return — as long as you do not request a refund of your contributions. If you take a refund, your service credit for that period is forfeited (but can be repaid with interest if you return).

⚠
Never take a contribution refund casually. Cashing out for a temporary gap forfeits years of compounding pension benefit. The cost to repurchase later is typically much higher than the refund you received.

Retiree Healthcare Benefits

Healthcare in retirement is often worth more than the pension itself. The rules changed dramatically based on your hire date — and most people have no idea where they stand.

1
What healthcare am I entitled to in retirement? Critical
+

Your retiree healthcare benefit depends entirely on your hire date:

Hire DateHealthcare Structure
Before July 1, 2008Premium Subsidy — up to 90% of premium paid by MPSERS after 30 years of service
July 1, 2008 – Sept. 3, 2012Graded Premium Subsidy — 30% at 10 YOS + 4% per additional year, max 80% at 25 YOS
Sept. 4, 2012 or laterPersonal Healthcare Fund (PHF) only — no premium subsidy at retirement
⚠
If you were hired on or after September 4, 2012, you have NO retiree healthcare premium subsidy. Healthcare costs in retirement must be fully self-funded. This is one of the largest planning gaps for recent hires — and the one that catches people most off guard.
2
What is the Personal Healthcare Fund (PHF)?
+

The PHF is an individual account funded by mandatory employee contributions (2% of salary) for members hired on or after September 4, 2012. It is managed through Voya Financial. Contributions are pre-tax and accumulate with investment growth.

If you have PHF, you cannot enroll in MPSERS group insurance after retirement — you use your PHF balance to purchase your own insurance. Monitor your PHF balance in miAccount alongside your pension projection.

3
Does my spouse get healthcare coverage in retirement?
+

If you have the premium subsidy benefit, your spouse and eligible dependents can be covered at the same subsidy percentage. However — if you choose the Straight Life payment option, your spouse's insurance coverage ends at your death. This interaction between your pension payment option and insurance continuity is a critical planning decision.

4
I was hired before 2012 — what about the 3% healthcare contribution I've been paying?
+

Under Public Act 127 of 2024, your school district is required to reimburse your 3% Retiree Healthcare Contribution deductions. This reimbursement began in FY 2025. PA 127 also eliminated the 3% contribution requirement for premium subsidy members beginning FY 2025–26.

✓
Significant positive news for pre-2012 hires. Confirm the timing and reimbursement process with your district's HR department and watch your pay stub.

Healthcare in retirement is the variable most people underestimate. An advisor can show you exactly what your situation looks like — what you'll have, what it'll cost, and how to plan for the gap.

When Can You Retire — and When Should You?

Eligibility and optimality are two different questions. Both matter enormously to your lifetime income.

ℹ
MIP members have the most favorable early retirement option in the system. A teacher who starts at 22 and teaches continuously could retire at 52 with a full pension. This is rare nationally.
2
What is the financial impact of working one extra year?
+

Each additional year provides three simultaneous pension boosts:

  • One additional year of service credit — adds 1.5% (MIP) or 1.25% of FAC to your annual pension
  • Potentially higher FAC — if your final year salary is higher than the lowest year in your averaging period
  • Shorter payout period — actuarial adjustment is smaller

Example: A MIP member earning $80,000 gains approximately $1,200/year in permanent pension income by working one additional year — $24,000 over a 20-year retirement.

3
When is the best month or date to retire?
+
  • End of fiscal year (September 30) — retiring September 30 vs. October 1 can mean one full extra year of service credit
  • FAC window — staying through a salary increase to include it in your averaging period can materially increase your pension
  • Healthcare enrollment — plan the gap so you're not uninsured between retirement and first coverage
  • Sick/vacation day payout — check your district contract; payout before retirement may affect your FAC

ORS recommends submitting your application at least 3–4 months before your intended effective date.

Payment Options — The Irrevocable Choice

How you elect to receive your pension permanently affects your monthly income and what your family receives after you're gone. This decision cannot be changed after you retire.

1
What payment options are available? Irrevocable
+

100% SurvivorReduced pension; full amount continues to beneficiaryLower monthly amount while alive

2
What is "pension maximization" and does it make sense?
+

Pension maximization means taking the Straight Life option and using the difference in monthly income to fund a life insurance policy that replaces lost income for your spouse at your death.

⚠
This strategy has real risks. If health issues make insurance prohibitively expensive, you're locked into Straight Life with no safety net. Evaluate carefully with an independent advisor — not someone selling insurance products.

The payment option decision is permanent. It's one of the few retirement choices where getting it wrong has no correction available. This is where a qualified advisor earns their worth.

Taxes on Your MPSERS Pension

Michigan's pension tax has been changing. Understanding federal and state treatment is essential to projecting your actual net retirement income.

1
Is my MPSERS pension taxed federally?
+

Yes. Your MPSERS pension is subject to federal income tax. It is reported on Form 1099-R and treated as ordinary income. There is no federal exemption for state government pensions. You can elect federal withholding through ORS or pay estimated taxes quarterly.

2
Is my pension taxed by the state of Michigan?
+

Michigan's treatment depends on your birth year, under Public Acts 4 and 5 of 2023 (the "Retirement Tax Rollback"):

Birth YearMichigan Tax Treatment
Born before 1946Fully exempt from Michigan income tax
Born 1946–1952Pension subtraction up to $20,000 (single) / $40,000 (joint); phasing to full exemption by 2026
Born 1953–1961Phased exemption being restored — fully exempt by 2026 tax year
Born 1962 or laterCurrently subject to Michigan income tax — confirm with a tax advisor
ℹ
Michigan's pension tax rules have been in flux. Always confirm current treatment with a tax professional or the Michigan Department of Treasury before finalizing retirement income projections.
3
How do I manage withholding on my pension check?
+

You can set federal and Michigan state withholding amounts when you submit your retirement application, and update them anytime through miAccount or by submitting a new W-4P / MI-W4P form to ORS.

If you have other income sources in retirement (Social Security, 403(b)/457 withdrawals, part-time work), have a tax professional model your total estimated tax obligation to avoid underpayment penalties.

Taxes in retirement are often an afterthought — until the first 1099-R arrives. Understanding your net pension income before you retire changes how you plan everything else.

Your Retirement Action Plan

Concrete steps organized by career stage. The earlier you start, the more options you have.

First 90 Days of Employment

  • Confirm your plan enrollment — log into miAccount and verify you are in the plan you intended.
  • Make your irrevocable plan election if within your 75-day window — the most important financial decision of your public school career. Seek guidance before the deadline passes.
  • Designate beneficiaries — in miAccount for both your DB contributions and DC account (if applicable).
  • Start 403(b) or 457 contributions — even small amounts compounded over decades become substantial. Don't wait.

Annually Throughout Your Career

  • Review your annual member statement — verify service credit is accurately recorded. Report discrepancies to ORS immediately.
  • Track your hours if you work part-time — confirm you're on track to reach 1,020 hours for a full year of service credit.
  • Update beneficiary designations after any major life event — marriage, divorce, birth of a child.
  • Increase 403(b)/457 contributions with every salary increase.
  • Consider service credit purchases — run the TDP Calculator to evaluate whether purchasing prior service is a good investment.

5 Years Before Retirement

  • Run pension estimates at multiple retirement dates in miAccount — quantify the impact of working each additional year.
  • Model your Social Security benefit at ages 62, 67, and 70 using SSA.gov — coordinate with your pension start date.
  • Evaluate your healthcare situation — know whether you have premium subsidy or PHF, and what your costs will be before Medicare at 65.
  • Review payment option choices with your spouse — don't wait until application time.
  • Assess your total retirement income: pension + Social Security + supplemental savings. Identify the gap.
  • Consider long-term care insurance — optimal to purchase at ages 55–62, before health issues develop.

12 Months Before Retirement

  • Verify all service credit is accurately recorded — resolve discrepancies with ORS well before your application.
  • Complete any pending service credit purchases.
  • Confirm your FAC components — verify with HR which earnings are pensionable.
  • Determine your healthcare enrollment status — plan any coverage gap between retirement and Medicare.

3–4 Months Before Retirement

  • Submit your retirement application to ORS — don't wait until the last minute.
  • Make your payment option election — Straight Life, 100% Survivor, 50% Survivor, etc. This is irrevocable.
  • Gather required documents: birth certificate, marriage certificate (if electing survivor benefit), Medicare card (if 65+), bank account for direct deposit.
  • Set up tax withholding — federal and state. Don't underpay and face penalties.
  • Contact Voya if you have DC or 457 accounts to discuss distribution timing.

Choosing an Advisor — and Knowing the Difference

Michigan public school employees are one of the most heavily solicited groups in the financial services industry. Here's what separates a genuine advocate from someone working an angle.

⚠
This is not abstract. In 2022, the SEC charged Equitable Financial Life Insurance Company with providing misleading fee statements to approximately 1.4 million variable annuity holders — most of them public school teachers. In 2020, VALIC Financial Advisors was charged with concealing conflicts of interest when steering teachers toward higher-fee 403(b) products. The Department of Labor found violations in 70% of ERISA 403(b) enforcement investigations between 2010 and 2021. The people approaching you in school parking lots and teacher lounges are often not advisors. They are salespeople with a quota. Knowing the difference is worth real money over your career.
1
What is a fiduciary — and why does it matter more than anything else you ask?
+

A fiduciary is legally required to act in your best interest — not their firm's interest, not their commission structure's interest, yours. A non-fiduciary advisor only has to recommend products that are "suitable" for you. Suitable and best are very different standards in practice.

The complication: many advisors can describe themselves as fiduciaries in some contexts and not others. They may wear a fiduciary hat during the planning phase and a salesperson hat when recommending products — all in the same meeting.

⚠
Don't just ask "are you a fiduciary?" Ask this instead: "Are you willing to sign a written agreement acknowledging that you will act as my legal fiduciary for all advice you provide, at all times during our relationship?" A genuine fiduciary will say yes without hesitation. Watch for hedging, lengthy explanations, or a redirect to a different question. Those are answers.

You can verify whether an advisor is a Registered Investment Advisor (RIA) — which carries a legal fiduciary obligation — through the SEC's Investment Adviser Public Disclosure website at adviserinfo.sec.gov. You can check any advisor's regulatory history through FINRA's BrokerCheck at brokercheck.finra.org.

2
How does this advisor actually get paid — and what does that mean for you?
+

Compensation structure is one of the most revealing things about an advisor. The three main models:

ModelHow They EarnConflict Risk
Fee-onlyFlat fee, hourly rate, or % of assets managed — no commissionsLow — they earn the same regardless of what they recommend
Fee-basedCombination of fees and commissionsModerate — commissions create product preference bias
Commission-onlyPaid when they sell you a productHigh — their income depends entirely on what you buy

When a "free" advisor shows up at your school or calls after a seminar, ask yourself: how do they get paid? The answer is almost always commissions on what they sell you. That's not necessarily wrong — but it means their recommendation isn't neutral, and you need to understand that going in.

⚠
Variable annuities inside 403(b) accounts have historically carried total annual fees of 1.8%–2.6%. A comparable mutual fund portfolio costs 0.05%–0.50%. On a $200,000 balance over 20 years, that fee difference can exceed $150,000 in lost growth. "No upfront fees" doesn't mean free.
3
Do they actually specialize in MPSERS — or are they a generalist who works with anyone?
+

MPSERS is a complex, Michigan-specific system with multiple tiers, unique healthcare provisions, specific FAC calculations, and retirement timing rules that don't apply anywhere else. A generalist financial advisor who works with small business owners, retirees, and teachers all in the same practice is not the same as someone who has spent years exclusively with Michigan public school employees.

Questions to determine real MPSERS specialization:

  • Can you explain the difference between MIP and Pension Plus 2 retirement eligibility rules off the top of your head?
  • What percentage of your current clients are Michigan public school employees?
  • Have you walked clients through the 75-day plan election decision? What do you typically recommend, and why?
  • How do you factor the retiree healthcare subsidy (or PHF) into a retirement income projection?
  • Can you explain the COLA situation for MPSERS retirees and what that means for supplemental savings strategy?
  • Have you worked with clients navigating MPSERS through a divorce — specifically with an EDRO?

A specialist answers these fluently. A generalist will talk around them. You'll know the difference immediately.

4
How often will they meet with you — and what does an ongoing relationship actually look like?
+

One of the most common complaints teachers have about financial advisors: they were enthusiastic during the sale and disappeared after. A legitimate ongoing relationship looks different from a transactional one.

What a real ongoing advisory relationship includes:

  • At minimum one annual review — revisiting your pension projection, supplemental savings progress, and any life changes that affect the plan
  • Proactive outreach when things change — legislative changes to MPSERS, contribution limit increases, new tax rules — your advisor should be telling you, not the other way around
  • Accessible between meetings — a direct phone number or email, with a clear expectation of response time. Not a call center.
  • Coordination as you approach retirement — more frequent contact in the 3–5 years before retirement when the decisions compound

Ask directly: "After our initial planning work is done, how often will we meet? Who specifically will I be talking to — you, or someone on your team? What's the best way to reach you if something comes up between meetings?"

ℹ
If the answer involves a client service team, a shared inbox, or an appointment system with a two-week lead time for routine questions — that's the level of access you're buying. Decide if that works for you before you sign anything.
5
How do they help you execute a retirement plan — not just create one?
+

A plan that sits in a drawer is not a plan. The difference between a good advisor and a great one is often execution — the discipline to actually implement what was recommended and adjust it as life changes.

Questions about execution capability:

  • Can you help me set up and manage my 403(b) and/or 457 contributions, or do you just advise and I figure out implementation?
  • If I need to purchase service credit, can you help me evaluate and execute a Tax-Deferred Payment arrangement?
  • When I'm within 12 months of retirement, what does your process look like for walking me through the ORS application, payment option election, and healthcare enrollment?
  • Can you coordinate with my accountant on pension withholding and estimated tax planning?
  • If I have DC or 457 accounts with Voya, can you help me optimize the investment allocation within those accounts?

An advisor who only produces a plan and hands it to you is half a service. Look for someone who stays involved through implementation.

6
How do they handle unexpected situations — divorce, disability, serious illness, death of a spouse?
+

Life rarely unfolds on schedule, and MPSERS has specific rules that interact badly with unexpected events if you're not prepared for them.

Divorce: Your MPSERS pension is marital property and can be divided through an Eligible Domestic Relations Order (EDRO). A generalist attorney may not know MPSERS EDRO rules — improperly drafted orders can create unintended outcomes or be rejected by ORS entirely. Ask your advisor: have you worked with clients through MPSERS divorces, and can you refer me to an attorney who knows EDRO requirements specifically?

Disability: MPSERS has disability benefit provisions, but they interact with your tier, vesting status, and Social Security disability in complex ways. Your advisor should know the basics and be able to refer you to a specialist if this situation arises.

Death of a spouse: If you elected a survivor payment option, your pension continues. If you didn't, it stops. Your advisor should have walked you through this scenario during the planning phase — before retirement, not after.

Serious illness before retirement: This can force an earlier-than-planned retirement. Your advisor should be able to model what an early retirement means for your benefit, your healthcare situation, and your Social Security strategy — on short notice if necessary.

⚠
Ask any advisor directly: "If I had to retire unexpectedly due to a health situation in the next 12 months, could you help me navigate that quickly and minimize the financial impact?" Their confidence and specificity in answering will tell you a lot.

8
Red flags — things that should end the conversation immediately.
+

Walk away from any advisor who does any of the following:

  • Approaches you unsolicited at school — legitimate advisors don't need to cold-call in teacher lounges. The ones who do are typically product salespeople with district access agreements.
  • Can't clearly explain how they get paid — this is not a complex question. If the answer is vague or requires multiple follow-ups, that's intentional.
  • Recommends a variable annuity inside your 403(b) without explaining the total fee structure — ask for the total annual cost including mortality and expense fees, fund expense ratios, and any administrative charges. If they can't give you a single number, that's a red flag.
  • Tells you to roll your 403(b) into a new product without discussing surrender charges — many annuity contracts have 7–10 year surrender periods with 8% penalties for early withdrawal.
  • Can't explain your MPSERS tier or doesn't ask which tier you're in — if they don't know what tier you're in within the first conversation, they don't know enough about MPSERS to help you.
  • Discourages you from getting a second opinion — a confident, legitimate advisor welcomes scrutiny.
  • Creates urgency ("this offer expires" or "rates are changing") — retirement planning decisions should never be made under artificial time pressure.
  • Has disciplinary actions on their BrokerCheck record — not every complaint is valid, but multiple customer complaints about suitability or disclosure are serious signals.
9
The complete checklist — questions to ask any advisor before you commit.
+

Use this checklist in any first meeting with an advisor — including anyone referred to you through this site. No legitimate advisor should be uncomfortable with any of these questions.

About Their Obligations

  • Are you a fiduciary 100% of the time? Will you put that in writing?
  • Are you registered as an RIA with the SEC or state? Can I verify that?
  • Do you have any current or past disciplinary actions on your BrokerCheck record?

About How They Get Paid

  • Are you fee-only, fee-based, or commission-based?
  • If you recommend a product, do you receive a commission? How much?
  • What is your total annual compensation from a client like me, across all sources?

About Their MPSERS Experience

  • What percentage of your clients are Michigan public school employees?
  • Can you explain the difference between MIP and Pension Plus 2 retirement eligibility?
  • How do you incorporate retiree healthcare — subsidy or PHF — into a retirement income plan?
  • Have you worked with clients through MPSERS plan elections, service credit purchases, or EDROs?

About the Ongoing Relationship

  • How often will we meet after the initial plan is complete?
  • Who specifically will I be talking to — you, or someone on your team?
  • How do I reach you between meetings, and what's your typical response time?
  • What does your process look like in the 12 months before a client retires?

About What Could Go Wrong

  • If I had to retire unexpectedly due to illness, how would you help me navigate that?
  • Have you worked with clients through a divorce involving an MPSERS pension?
  • If I'm in a 403(b) annuity with surrender charges, how do you factor that into your recommendations?
  • What happens to my plan if something happens to you — who takes over my relationship?
✓
A good advisor will answer every one of these questions directly, without irritation or deflection. An advisor who gets defensive about any of them is showing you something important. Pay attention to it.
